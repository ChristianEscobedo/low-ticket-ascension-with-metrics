he Six Design Principles
The principles that govern every decision in the methodology. When something feels off, return here.
1. Diagnosis before prescription. She cannot redesign what she has not named. Phase 0 and Week 1 are diagnostic, not prescriptive.
2. Subtraction before addition. The methodology removes before it adds. The first systems we install take work off her plate. The anti-hustle position, operationalized.
3. One domain at a time, in a deliberate order. Mental load first because nothing else works until her head is clear. Then time. Then household. Then identity. The order is the work.
4. Systems she keeps, not lessons she watches. By Day 90 she walks away with installed systems running her life. Not insights. Systems.
5. Public and private work. Alone work installs the system. Room work installs the identity. The combination creates transformation.
6. The 90 days is the foundation, not the finish. The 90-day arc is the initiation into the redesign. After Day 90 she enters the Ongoing Practice. The OS is a permanent membership.
The Redesign Method

Days 1–3. The map she did not know she needed.
Before she begins the work, she maps her life across ten domains, scored 0 to 10. Higher is always better. This becomes the reference point for every decision that follows: what to subtract, what to redesign, what to claim in the Rewrite.

THE MOTHERMODE INDEX
Her ten scores are averaged (unweighted) into a single composite number from 0.0 to 10.0. This is her MotherMode Index. It moves with seasons, with life events, with the work she does. Individual Indexes are always private. Cohort aggregates are shared. App users complete the Wheel once at Day 0. Ongoing Index tracking lives in the OS — the natural upgrade pressure.
The Three-Phase Arc
The shape of the foundational quarter. 90 days. Three phases of 30 days each. Reclamation → Rebuild → Rewrite. The emotional arc is relief, then control, then expansion.
Phase 1 — The Reclamation (Days 4–30). She gets her head back. Mental load named, externalized, time audited, the first cut made. This phase is about relief. End-of-phase milestone: she has reclaimed her first 5 to 10 hours per week.
Phase 2 — The Rebuild (Days 31–60). With her head clear, she rebuilds the operating systems of her life. Household. Routines. Domains. The partner conversation lives here, framed as a structural redesign, not a relationship intervention. This phase is about construction. End-of-phase milestone: her life is running on systems, not willpower.
Phase 3 — The Rewrite (Days 61–90). She authors the next version of her identity. Two layers: the personal rewrite (who she is becoming, on purpose) and the generational rewrite (contributing to the cultural redesign of motherhood). This phase is about expansion.
The Ongoing Practice
What happens after Day 90. The methodology does not end — it becomes the rhythm of her life.
Weekly Gathering (90 min, every week, Loni leads): teach, Hot Seat, Stage Breakouts by quarter, closing.
Stage Breakouts: members split by quarter (Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4, Architect). Same stage, fluid composition. Small-group intimacy without permanent pods.
Monthly Redesigns: 90-minute deep-dives on a specific domain. Architect members lead some.
Quarterly Reclamation Challenges: 30-day cohort challenges with a defined outcome. Brand-led, seasonal.
The Retreat: Once a year. Invite-only. $5,000. Executive restoration for mothers.
Regional Meetups: Twice a year. Stage-organized. Hosted by Loni or Architect-tier ambassadors.

Brand Voice and Tone


THE ONE-SENTENCE VOICE DEFINITION
MotherMode sounds like a brilliant, slightly tired, deeply loving woman texting her smartest friend the truth at 11pm. With the calm authority of someone who has already figured out what she’s going to do about it.
The Five Voice Pillars
Every piece of MotherMode writing must hit all five. Not three of five. All five.
1. Honest before flattering. MotherMode tells the truth even when the truth is inconvenient. It doesn’t shout. We never use the comforting lie when the uncomfortable truth would actually help.
2. Unapologetic, calibrated, not extreme. We say sharp things and immediately calibrate. “Motherhood sucks. Not all of it. Not always. But enough of it that you’ve thought it.” The calibration is what separates us from a hashtag.
3. Premium without being precious or pretentious. We sound expensive. We do not sound frilly, fluffy, or fragile. No bath-bomb language. No glow-up speak. The brand earns trust by not performing warmth, and being warm anyway. We are accessible and never elitist. 
4. Generational, not just personal or preachy. The brand always remembers it’s playing a long game. Even when we’re talking about today’s mental load, the subtext is the larger redesign of motherhood.
5. Confident, not corporate or saccharine. When MotherMode shows up, the reader feels seen, not coddled. We sound like a woman who knows what she’s doing. No buzzwords. Plain, sharp, deliberate language with an unmistakable point of view.
THE VOICE EQUATION
MotherMode = Truth + Calibration + Permission + Mission
Every piece should have at least three of these. The strongest pieces have all four.

The Voice Tests
Before publishing anything, run these four.
The Loni Test. Could Loni Brown actually say this out loud, in her own voice, to a friend at dinner?
The Avatar Test. Read it imagining the avatar at 11pm, exhausted, scrolling. Does she feel seen, or sold to?
The Daughter Test. Imagine your daughters reading this in 20 years. Would you be proud of every word? This test catches anything that’s clever-but-cynical, edgy-but-empty, or trendy-but-shallow.
The Whitespace Test. Could you delete 30% of the words and the meaning would get stronger? MotherMode writes tight.
Hard Lines (Non-Negotiable)
A few things MotherMode never does. These are non-negotiable, and they’re worth printing and putting on the wall.
Never punch down at fathers, partners, or non-mothers. The brand’s enemy is the system, not men, not childless women, not stay-at-home moms, not working moms, not any group of women. We fight a structure, not a population.
Never weaponize children. Don’t use kids as guilt-bait or pity-bait in copy. The brand respects motherhood too much to use it as marketing leverage.
Never sell from fear. No “if you don’t fix this, you’ll regret it forever” copy. Sell from possibility, sell from truth, sell from permission. Never from terror.
Never apologize for ambition. The brand never softens the woman’s right to want more. Permission is loud.
Never perform motherhood. No “mama bear” energy, no “warrior mom” energy, no “boss mom” energy. The brand respects mothers too much to caricature them.
Never sound like a wellness influencer. No “high vibes,” no “manifest your best life,” no “energy alignment,” no astrology-adjacent metaphysical language. MotherMode is grounded in systems and truth, not vibes.
Never trade depth for clicks. The brand is allowed to be sharp, but never shallow. If a line is just provocative without being true, cut it.
Never break the calibration rule. Every bold claim gets calibrated within 1–2 sentences. We earn the right to say hard things by always saying the full truth, not just the inflammatory half.

The Brand Color: “Mode”
The brand’s signature aubergine (#532B3C) is named “Mode.” It’s a custom color, blended between cabernet and aubergine to feel warm enough to be maternal, deep enough to be unapologetic, and premium enough to be editorial. When designers and copywriters reference the color in style guides, they say “Mode aubergine” or “set in Mode.”
Color Usage Rules
RULE 1: 60-30-10
Most layouts should be 60% Bone, 30% Deep Charcoal, 10% Mode aubergine. The accent earns its impact by being rare, not dominant.
RULE 2: MODE BELONGS TO THE BRAND, NOT THE PRODUCT
Use Mode aubergine for brand moments (logo, hero headline, manifesto, merch). The product UI should lean almost entirely on Bone and Charcoal so the brand color stays special when it appears.
RULE 3: NEVER PASTEL
No pale pink, mint, lavender, or pastel versions of any palette color. The palette stays at full saturation. Pastels would betray the brand’s unapologetic positioning.
RULE 4: BRASS IS THE SPICE
Aged Brass (#A88B5C) appears in maybe 5% of brand surfaces. Reserved for premium-only moments: membership materials, eventual book cover, conference signage, premium merch.

Typography


THE PAIR
Display face: Tiempos Headline. A modern serif with editorial weight. Used by NYT, premium publications, and serious literary brands. When MotherMode’s manifesto is set in Tiempos, it doesn’t look like marketing copy. It looks like literature.
Body and UI: Inter. The gold-standard tech sans of 2026. Free, comprehensive, beautifully designed, and ubiquitous in premium tech (GitHub, Figma, Linear). Signals “modern infrastructure” without trying too hard. Legible at every size.
Why this pair
Together they create the exact tension MotherMode needs: editorial soul + modern infrastructure. The body copy looks like a tech company. The headlines look like a book. That contrast is the brand made visible.
Typography Rules
Headlines in Tiempos Headline. Tight tracking. Generous line height. Editorial cadence requires room to breathe.
Body and UI in Inter, regular weight, line height 1.5 to 1.7. Premium tech brands always use generous line height.
No more than two type families. A third (script, decorative, novelty) instantly cheapens the brand.
All caps used sparingly. Reserved for category labels, navigation, small editorial flourishes. Never for headlines or emphasis.
No drop shadows, no gradients on type, no novelty effects.
Italics used for emphasis or quotes only. Never for decoration.

The Three Photography Modes
MODE 1: THE EDITORIAL PORTRAIT (HIGH-STAKES BRAND MOMENTS)
Magazine-grade photographs of real mothers, shot in their homes or styled environments. Used for homepage hero, manifesto, press kit, eventual book cover. Direct eye contact. Confident posture. Warm light. Treats her as the subject, never the object.
MODE 2: THE REAL MOMENT (FOUNDER CONTENT, SOCIAL, MEMBER STORIES)
Documentary-feeling photography of unposed moments. Hands wrapped around a coffee cup. View of the school pickup line through a windshield. Pile of shoes by the door. Slightly grainy texture is fine. Imperfection is the point.
MODE 3: THE QUIET OBJECT (PRODUCT MOMENTS, APP SCREENSHOTS, LIFESTYLE STILLS)
Beautiful still life of the things in her life. Coffee mug. Notebook. Phone. A single hand. A flower in a glass. The corner of a window. These exist between the people and become the brand’s signature compositional move.
What MotherMode Never Shows
Stock photography of “a mom” (any photo that feels like it could appear on twenty other websites)
Perfectly happy “supermom” smiling while balancing a baby and a laptop
Pastel backgrounds with cursive overlay quotes
“Mom and kids” as the central subject (this is a brand for her, not for “mom-and-kids” as a unit)
Sunset gradients, sunburst graphics, mandala overlays, or any “feminine empowerment” visual cliché
Hands holding coffee that say “but first, coffee”
Wine glass imagery (the “wine mom” trope is one of MotherMode’s enemies)
Babies as cuteness props
Anything that uses motherhood as decoration rather than subject
Aspirational lifestyle that feels unattainable (the brand is real, not Pinterest-fantasy)
Illustration Usage
MotherMode uses illustration sparingly and editorially. The brand is photo-led, not illustration-led. When illustration appears, it follows these rules:
Single-color line art only (in Mode aubergine, Deep Charcoal, or Aged Brass)
Editorial in style (think New Yorker spot illustration, not bouncy emoji)
Used for diagrams, in-app icons, conceptual moments where photography won’t work
Never used to depict women’s faces or bodies — always concepts, objects, or abstract forms
Hand-drawn quality preferred over geometric/perfect
Most of the brand stays in photography. Illustration is the spice, not the dish.
Children’s Privacy
Never show the faces of Loni’s daughters in any MotherMode content. Backs of heads or backs in pictures are okay. Names are not used in public copy. Apply the 18-year filter: would your daughter at 18 be okay with this being the first thing Google reveals about her?
The Anti-Moodboard
MotherMode is consciously NOT:
Pinterest mom blog (cursive, mason jars, “but first coffee”)
Productivity app (Notion, Linear cold geometry)
Wellness app (Calm, Headspace pastel meditation)
Wine-mom (sarcastic mug graphics)
Girlboss (pink everywhere, cursive empowerment)
Crunchy mom (essential oils visual world)
Influencer-mom (over-styled, performative, Stanley-cup curated)
Subscription-box (pastel packaging, treat-yo-self energy)
